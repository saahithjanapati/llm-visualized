Dream Machines

On human-computer interaction and the renaissance already underway.

Look back at the history of computing and you find one relentless pattern: the friction between what you want and what the machine does keeps shrinking. Punch cards gave way to the command line. The command line gave way to the graphical user interface. The desktop GUI eventually gave way to smartphones that fit in your pocket. At each step, more people could harness the power that computers had to offer.

01

AI is accelerating this trend in a way that feels categorically different from anything before it, because two forces are operating at once, and they are feeding each other. The underlying technology is advancing at a pace that has no historical precedent. And that same technology is enabling entirely new interfaces that collapse the gap between human intention and machine execution.

It's not just that our tools are becoming more powerful, it's that the interfaces to those tools are becoming as expressive as human language and thought itself.

Developers who once wrote code line by line now direct teams of Codex agents with descriptions of what they want to build. Social media and news feeds traditionally have relied on sparse, low-bandwith signals (a like, a pause, a scroll), but products like ChatGPT Pulse let you describe what you want to see in natural language, and the system shapes itself accordingly. The machines, at long last, are learning to meet us where we are.

02

As a result, more people than ever before are able to harness capabilities once reserved for a select few. And that shift matters, because the most consequential question in the history of technology has never just been what computers can do, but also who gets to use them and how*.

We are on the cusp of a renaissance, one where human creativity and ingenuity isn't replaced by technology, but amplified by it.

Everyone is getting new superpowers. Everyone is getting new colors to paint their ideas with.

*This framing may soon require revision. As AI systems begin conducting science and pushing the boundaries of knowledge autonomously, "What computers can do" could itself become the most consequential question. But even in that world, perhaps especially in that world, the project of building products and interfaces that enrich human experience and expand who gets to participate feels more important, not less.
